About

Buildings
that breathe

Stucco Stories is a visual exploration of the built environment — from the grandeur of monumental structures to the quiet intimacy of a well-worn interior. Based in Chennai, a city layered with colonial, Dravidian, and modern architecture.

The work documents architecture, interiors, materials, and texture — always through the lens of natural light and honest observation. No staging. No styling. Just spaces as they are.

Every crack in a wall, every patch of afternoon light on plaster, every shadow thrown by a column — these are the stories worth telling.

Why Doors Warp

A visual story of what happens inside your door when Chennai's monsoon arrives — and what actually stops it.

Solid teak · Flush doors IS 2202 · IS 1341 · BIS 2024–25 Chennai climate context
1

What does a warped door look like?

The door still opens. The hinges are fine. But something is clearly wrong.

Warping is when the flat face of a door bends out of its plane. The top and bottom stay fixed at the hinges — the middle bows outward. The latch stops aligning. The door starts rubbing the frame. Left long enough, it will not close at all.

Elevation view — same door, same hinges, same frame. The bow is emphasised for clarity; in practice 15–25mm is enough to prevent the latch from catching.

The warped door hasn't failed structurally. The hinges still hold. But it has become unusable. In Chennai, this typically shows within the first two monsoon seasons on an untreated or under-supported door.

2

The moisture story — why it happens

Wood breathes with the weather, even after it's been cut and finished.

Think of the grain like a bundle of drinking straws running from top to bottom. When monsoon hits, the outer face absorbs moisture — those straws swell sideways. The inner face, sheltered and sealed, stays the same size. One side grows longer than the other. The door has no choice but to curve toward the drier face.

This happens to both solid teak and flush doors — but for slightly different reasons and with different consequences. The monsoon doesn't distinguish between them.

The one step most carpenters skip: Seal all four edges with exterior PU finish before the door goes into the frame. The bottom edge especially — moisture from the floor wicks up through end grain, travels up the stile, and causes veneer to lift at the corners within two monsoon seasons. Five minutes of sealing prevents years of problems.
3

What hinges actually do

More hinges don't make a door stronger. They make it more constrained.

The top and bottom hinges are the only structural ones — always. The top holds in tension, the bottom in compression. Together they carry the full weight. Every hinge in between does one job only: stops the middle of the door from bowing outward.

4

Inside a flush door — three core constructions

Every flush door looks the same from outside. Cut one open and you find something completely different inside.

The core — the material packed inside the outer timber frame — determines weight, warp resistance, screw-holding, and longevity. IS 2202 was updated in 2022–23 and mandates ISI marking on all flush doors sold in India from February 2025.

Hollow core
IS 2191 · 8–14 kg

Empty frame inside. Lightest, cheapest. Frame itself can twist in humidity.

Warps / racks easily
Blockboard core
IS 2202 Pt.1 · 20–30 kg

Solid timber blocks packed side by side. Adjacent grain opposes — self-stabilising. Most common in India.

Best warp resistanceStrong screw hold
MDF / particle board core
IS 2202 Pt.2 · 25–35 kg

Engineered fill — no grain direction so it doesn't bow. But swells uniformly in moisture. Heaviest option.

No directional bowSwells in moisture
Always specify BWP grade — not MR. Boiling Water Proof grade uses phenol formaldehyde resin and survives Chennai's coastal humidity. MR grade will delaminate within 2–3 monsoons in any exposed area. From February 2025, all flush doors sold in India must carry the ISI mark.
5

The one thing most people miss

You can have the right door, the right grade, the right hinge count — and still get warping.

Sealing all four edges of the door before it goes into the frame takes five minutes and prevents the majority of warping. The bottom edge is the most critical: moisture from the floor wicks up through the end grain, travels up the stile, and causes veneer to lift at the corners within two monsoons.

Apply two coats of exterior PU finish to all four cut edges before the door goes into the frame. A five-minute step that prevents years of problems.

In summary: Warping is a moisture physics problem, not a hinge problem. The right hinge count constrains the bow once it starts — but edge sealing and BWP grade prevent moisture from entering in the first place. Do all three and a door in Chennai's climate will last a generation.
IS 2202:2022–23 · IS 2191:2022 · IS 1341:2018 · BIS QCO 2024–25 For reference use only — verify with current BIS documentation
